## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### 1. The Technology Adoption Life Cycle (TALC)
Five psychographic groups respond differently to **discontinuous (disruptive) innovation**:

| Innovators (2.5%) | Early Adopters (13.5%) | Early Majority (34%) | Late Majority (34%) | Laggards (16%) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Techies | **Visionaries** | **Pragmatists** | Conservatives | Skeptics |
| Buy technology for its own sake | Buy 10× strategic leaps | Buy productivity improvements | Buy established standards | Don't buy |

Between every pair of groups is a *gap*. The gap between **visionaries and pragmatists is the chasm** — the most dangerous transition in high-tech market development, because the basis of sale is fundamentally different on each side.

**Operative test for a "market" (Moore's definition)**: a self-referencing set of customers buying the same product for the same need. *If your "market" can't reference each other, it's not a market — it's a list.*

### 2. The Two Sides of the Chasm

| Visionaries buy… | Pragmatists buy… |
|---|---|
| A **change agent** — radical discontinuity | A **productivity improvement** — minimal disruption |
| On intuition, no references | Only with strong vertical references |
| Custom projects, willing to debug | Standardized products that just work |
| Value-based pricing, price-insensitive | Competition-based pricing, ~30% leader premium |
| **You** sell the cool product | **You** must deliver a complete whole product |

The catch-22: the only credible pragmatist reference is another pragmatist — but no pragmatist will buy until several have already adopted. Breaking this loop is the central problem of crossing the chasm.

### 3. The D-Day Strategy
**Concentrate overwhelming force on a single beachhead niche. Dominate it. Then expand.**

Beachhead must be:
- **Big enough to matter** (real revenue)
- **Small enough to win** (too small for the incumbent to defend hard)
- **Good fit with your crown jewels** (leverages what you uniquely do)

**Crucial**: niche size is *not* the criterion — *pain magnitude is*. Documentum crossed the chasm by targeting ~40 pharma regulatory-affairs departments, each losing $1M/day in patent life. Small niche, unbearable pain → fastest exit.

**You cannot cross the chasm in two places.** Pick one beachhead. Commit everything.

### 4. The Whole Product Model (Levitt → Moore)
Four concentric rings:
1. **Generic product** — what you ship
2. **Expected product** — minimum config customer thinks they're buying
3. **Augmented product** — maximum support for the buying objective
4. **Potential product** — third-party + customization extensions

**"Pragmatists evaluate and buy whole products."** Once 2+ generic products exist, R&D on the generic product yields diminishing returns; the battle moves to whole-product completeness. In the chasm, *you* must assemble the whole product (visionaries did it themselves pre-chasm; third parties will do it post-chasm).

### 5. The Market Development Strategy Checklist (Beachhead Scoring)
Score each candidate niche 1–5 on 9 factors.

**Showstoppers** (any low score = disqualify):
1. Target Customer (single identifiable economic buyer)
2. **Compelling Reason to Buy** (dominant tiebreaker — pain so severe they can't wait another year)
3. Whole Product (fieldable in 3 months, dominant in 12)
4. Competition (no one has crossed the chasm here ahead of you)

**Nice-to-haves** (recoverable):
5. Partners and Allies
6. Distribution
7. Pricing
8. Positioning
9. Next Target Customer (bowling-alley potential)

### 6. Create the Competition + Two-Sentence Positioning
Pragmatists refuse to buy without a visible competitive set. If none exists, you must construct one:
- **Market alternative**: the incumbent the customer already buys from (defines the budget you preempt).
- **Product alternative**: another disruptor riding the same tech wave (legitimates the wave; you differentiate by segment focus).

**Master positioning template**:
> For [target customer — beachhead only] who is dissatisfied with [market alternative], our product is a [category] that provides [compelling reason to buy]. Unlike [product alternative], we have assembled [whole product features for your specific application].

If you can't say it during one elevator ride, you have no claim, no word-of-mouth, no investable strategy, no committed partners.

### 7. The Competitive Positioning Compass — 4 Quadrants
| | Specialist audience | Generalist audience |
|---|---|---|
| Skeptical | "Name it and frame it" (tech enthusiast) | **"Competition and differentiation"** (pragmatist) ← chasm crossing |
| Supportive | "Who for and what for" (visionary) | "Financials and futures" (conservative) |

**Crossing the chasm = pivot from product-centric value language to market-centric language.**
- Product-centric: cool product, easy to use, elegant architecture, unique functionality.
- Market-centric: complete whole product, solid UX, standards compatibility, fit for purpose.

### 8. Distribution & Pricing in the Chasm
**Distribution is the #1 priority** — before revenues, before profit, before press. Match channel to customer:

| Customer | Channel |
|---|---|
| Enterprise execs | Direct sales |
| End users | Web self-service / freemium |
| Dept managers | Sales 2.0 (digital direct-touch) |
| Design engineers | Two-tier distribution |
| Small biz owners | Value-Added Reseller (VAR) |

**Pricing during the chasm exists to motivate the channel**, not to satisfy customer or investor:
- Reset from value-based to **competition-based** (~30% leader premium).
- Add a **channel-motivation premium** — disproportionate margin, phased out as the product establishes itself.
- Vendor-oriented (cost+margin) pricing is the *weakest* basis during chasm — use only as a sanity check.

### 9. The Staircase, Not the Hockey Stick
Real high-tech revenue is a **staircase**: niche spurts + chasm/transition plateaus. Hockey-stick forecasts are spreadsheet fantasies that almost always destroy founder equity when they don't materialize. Pre-chasm commitments (equity grants, comp plans, partner contracts) tied to hockey-stick numbers become the post-chasm prison.

**Adopt profitability discipline early.** Visionary customers will prepay royalties and consulting fees. Cash-flow positive from Day 1 forces focus and preserves equity.
