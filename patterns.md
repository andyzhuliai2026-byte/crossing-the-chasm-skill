# Patterns — Chasm-Crossing Playbook

## Pattern: Diagnose the chasm
**When to use**: Your early-market success has stalled. Visionary pilots aren't converting to mainstream rollouts. Sales pipeline looks great but close rates dropped. Pragmatist prospects say "great presentation" and never buy.
**How**:
1. Map current customers to TALC segments (innovators / visionaries / pragmatists / conservatives / laggards).
2. Check: are visionaries saturated? Are pragmatists circling but not buying?
3. Test references — would a pragmatist consider your visionary customers as credible references? (Almost always no.)
4. If yes → you are in the chasm. Stop scaling, start the D-Day playbook.
**Trade-offs**: Diagnosing late = capital burn while ramping the wrong way. Diagnosing early = potentially shrinking the early market prematurely.

## Pattern: Beachhead selection
**When to use**: Entering chasm; need to pick one niche.
**How**:
1. Generate 20–50 one-page target customer scenarios (Header / Day-in-Life-Before / Day-in-Life-After / Compelling reason to buy).
2. Cluster to 8–10 distinct archetypes.
3. Score each on 4 *showstopper* factors (Target Customer / Compelling Reason to Buy / Whole Product / Competition), 1–5.
4. Eliminate any with a low score on any showstopper.
5. Score survivors on 5 *nice-to-have* factors (Partners, Distribution, Pricing, Positioning, Next Target Customer).
6. Commit to **one** beachhead. Communicate the commitment company-wide.
**Trade-offs**: Single-target focus forfeits optionality. The math says it's still better than hedging.

## Pattern: Compelling reason to buy
**When to use**: Validating any beachhead candidate, or diagnosing a stuck deal.
**How**: For each prospect, identify the *broken process* and quantify its cost per unit time (per day, per quarter). If the buyer cannot articulate a cost or a deadline, there is no compelling reason — only academic interest. Cure: switch the conversation from the product to the broken process, until pain is named and quantified.
**Trade-offs**: Pragmatists with no compelling reason will gladly meet you forever — they're educating themselves on your dime.

## Pattern: Whole product planning
**When to use**: After beachhead is selected; before launch.
**How**:
1. Write the target customer's "ideal day after" use case end-to-end.
2. List every product, service, integration, and skill they need.
3. For each item, assign a source: (a) your R&D, (b) acquisition, (c) partner, (d) alliance.
4. Build the partner ecosystem around *specific gaps*, not abstract synergies.
5. Set the 3-month-completion / 12-month-domination clock.
**Trade-offs**: Whole product completeness slows generic-product R&D. That's the right trade in chasm — pragmatists buy whole products.

## Pattern: Wire the marketplace
**When to use**: You have a beachhead + a whole product + a 10× value prop.
**How**: Make your product the only reasonable buying proposition in the beachhead by (a) framing the category to match your strengths, (b) defining the competitive set via market alternative + product alternative, (c) ensuring every whole-product element points to you as the natural assembler. (d) Recruit partners and analysts to echo this framing.
**Trade-offs**: Concentrated focus → easier for incumbent to recognize and counter; offset by speed of saturation.

## Pattern: Create the competition
**When to use**: Pragmatist prospects are skeptical because "this looks too new" or "we don't see other vendors here."
**How**:
1. Pick a **market alternative** (the incumbent they currently spend money with for the budget you want).
2. Pick a **product alternative** (another disruptor with the same new technology).
3. Position yourself as: "uses the same new tech wave (product alternative legitimates it), but specifically tuned to *our* segment, replacing budget historically spent on the market alternative."
**Trade-offs**: You give competitors free advertising. Worth it — without a credible competitive set, pragmatists won't buy at all.

## Pattern: Two-sentence positioning claim
**When to use**: Every major external communication; before any campaign launch.
**How**: Use the template
> For [target customer] who is dissatisfied with [market alternative], our product is a [category] that provides [compelling reason to buy]. Unlike [product alternative], we have assembled [whole product features].
Cut everything else.
**Trade-offs**: Forces you to sacrifice secondary claims. That sacrifice is the point.

## Pattern: Pass the elevator test
**When to use**: Pitching to VCs, executives, journalists, partners.
**How**: Reduce the positioning statement to two sentences a non-expert can repeat after hearing it once. If they can't, you have no claim, no word-of-mouth, and no investable strategy.
**Trade-offs**: Cuts deep technical nuance. Recover it in subsequent conversation, not in the opening.

## Pattern: Pricing the chasm crossing
**When to use**: Setting price as you enter mainstream.
**How**:
1. **Reset from value-based (visionary) to competition-based** (pragmatist) — typically ~30% premium over the market alternative if you can credibly claim leadership.
2. **Inject a channel-motivation premium** — disproportionate margin for the channel, phased out as the product establishes itself.
3. Use vendor-oriented (cost+margin) inputs only as a sanity check, not a primary driver.
**Trade-offs**: Loses early-market price-insensitive premium. Buys mainstream-channel pull-through.

## Pattern: Match the channel to the customer
**When to use**: Designing your go-to-market structure.
**How**: Pick from the five channels based on who actually buys:
- Enterprise execs ($100Ks–$Ms) → **Direct sales**.
- End users ($10s–$100s) → **Web self-service / freemium**.
- Dept managers (mid-market mid-cost) → **Sales 2.0**.
- Design engineers (component sales) → **Two-tier distribution**.
- Small business owner-operators → **VAR channel**.
**Trade-offs**: Mixing channels confuses prospects and creates conflict; pick one primary, supplement only when economics demand.

## Pattern: Bowling alley expansion
**When to use**: Beachhead has been dominated; planning next niches.
**How**:
1. Identify niches that share *either* customer word-of-mouth (same vertical) *or* partner ecosystem (same whole product) with the head pin.
2. Sequence them so each conquest seeds the next.
3. Apply the same chasm-crossing playbook to each — faster and cheaper than the first.
**Trade-offs**: Easy to confuse Bowling Alley with Tornado and over-scale. Confirm whole-product completeness before mass-market expansion.

## Pattern: Anticipate the tornado
**When to use**: Whole product has become generic; multiple sectors are starting to adopt at once.
**How**: Switch from niche-focused selling to platform / volume / share thinking. Push to #1 share via lowest-cost reach, partner pecking orders, switching-cost lock-in (the "gorilla" play).
**Trade-offs**: Niche margins evaporate; success now measured in absolute share, not in segment dominance.

## Pattern: Avoid the hockey-stick trap
**When to use**: Building business plan, raising capital, setting comp plans.
**How**:
1. Model revenue as a **staircase** — niche spurts + transition flats — not as a hockey stick.
2. Push back on VCs who demand hockey-stick forecasts; cite the chasm model and the staircase as the validated alternative.
3. Adopt **profitability discipline early** — even prepaid royalties / consulting fees can keep you cash-flow positive without diluting equity.
4. Don't lock in equity grants, comp plans, or partner contracts that assume hockey-stick numbers — they become the prison the post-chasm enterprise can't escape.
**Trade-offs**: Smaller paper valuation early; far more durable equity later.

## Pattern: Re-cast pioneers and settlers
**When to use**: Chasm is being crossed; org strain visible.
**How**:
1. Distinguish **pioneers** (improvisers, custom-deal closers, early-market heroes) from **settlers** (process-builders, scale operators, mainstream heroes).
2. Honor both; don't ask either to be the other.
3. Reassign roles deliberately — don't promote pioneers into roles they'll fail in.
4. Sometimes pioneers belong on the next innovation (next early market), not on the mainstream team.
**Trade-offs**: Tough conversations, sometimes departures. Worth it — mismatched roles destroy more chasm crossings than competitors do.

## Pattern: Consumer-digital pincer (Four Gears)
**When to use**: Product has both B2C grassroots and B2B institutional dimensions (health, education, civic services, prosumer SaaS).
**How**: Run Four Gears (Acquire → Engage → Enlist → Monetize, with Engagement *first*) for the consumer side AND a chasm-crossing playbook for the institutional side. Lead with consumer traction; let institutional sales capitalize on it.
**Trade-offs**: Two playbooks = two leadership teams = double the management complexity. Sometimes still the right call.
