# Glossary — Crossing the Chasm

**Augmented product** — The product fleshed out to provide the *maximum* chance of achieving the buying objective; includes ancillary products, services, and integrations beyond the expected baseline. (Ch5)

**Beachhead segment** — The single niche market chosen for the chasm crossing. Must be big enough to matter, small enough to win, and a good fit with your crown jewels. (Ch3, Ch4)

**Bowling alley** — The phase after chasm crossing where the head-pin niche knocks over adjacent niches in sequence; also the strategic logic of niche-by-niche market expansion. (Ch3, App. 1)

**Category** — An aggregate sales total across many self-referencing markets (e.g., "the oscilloscope market"). Useful for financial analysts; *not* an actionable marketing object. (Ch2)

**Chasm** — The deep, unrecognized gap between the early-adopter (visionary) and early-majority (pragmatist) segments of the Technology Adoption Life Cycle. The most dangerous transition in high-tech market development. (Ch1, Ch2)

**Compelling reason to buy** — Pain so severe the pragmatist customer cannot live with the problem another year. The dominant tiebreaker on the Market Development Strategy Checklist. (Ch4)

**Competitive Positioning Compass** — A 2×2 model: specialist vs. generalist audience × skeptical vs. supportive attitude. Maps four positioning stages (Name & Frame / Who-for-What-for / Competition & Differentiation / Financials & Futures). (Ch6)

**Competition-based pricing** — Pricing relative to a defined competitive set, typically with a ~30% premium for the market leader. Pragmatist-appropriate. (Ch7)

**Conservatives / Late majority** — The third-of-the-market psychographic that buys only when products are mature, standardized, and commoditized. Cost-based pricing. (Ch1, Ch2, Ch7)

**Continuous innovation** — A product upgrade that does not require behavior change or new supporting infrastructure. (Ch1)

**Cost-based pricing** — Pricing as a thin margin above cost, characteristic of late-mainstream / Main Street markets serving conservatives. (Ch7)

**Creating the competition** — In chasm-crossing, the act of constructing a visible competitive set using a *market alternative* (incumbent for the same budget) and a *product alternative* (another disruptor riding the same tech wave). (Ch6)

**D-Day strategy** — The book's master metaphor: concentrate overwhelming force on a single beachhead niche, dominate it, then expand. (Ch3)

**Design win** — When an engineer selects a component for inclusion in a product design, triggering a contract negotiation with purchasing. Front-end of two-tier distribution. (Ch7)

**Direct sales** — Top-down enterprise sales model (relationship marketing → solution selling → pro services delivery). Channel for enterprise-executive buyers. (Ch7)

**Discontinuous (disruptive) innovation** — A product that forces customers, and the surrounding infrastructure, to change behavior. The only type of innovation for which the TALC is needed. (Ch1)

**Early adopters / Visionaries** — Non-technologist buyers who pursue order-of-magnitude strategic advantage. Project-based, custom-work, price-insensitive. (Ch1, Ch2)

**Early market** — Pre-chasm phase dominated by innovators and visionaries; sold as projects via direct sales. (Ch1, Ch2, App. 1)

**Early majority / Pragmatists** — The ~34% segment that defines mainstream-market success. Reference-driven, risk-averse, market-leader-loyal, vertically networked within their industry. (Ch1, Ch2)

**Elevator test** — Can you state your positioning in the time of one elevator ride? Used by VCs as an investment filter. (Ch6)

**Expected product** — The *minimum* configuration of products and services the customer believes they are buying. (Ch5)

**Fad → Trend conversion** — The unique high-tech challenge: start as a fad, then convert into a sustainable trend. (Introduction)

**Flagship application** — A single, compelling use case that bridges the first crack (innovator → early adopter) by showing non-technologists what the technology enables. (Ch1)

**Four Gears Model** — Consumer-digital adoption model: Acquire → Engage → Enlist → Monetize. Engage first, monetize last. (App. 2)

**Freemium** — Web-self-service pricing model: give the core away, monetize via upsell to added-value features. (Ch7)

**Generic product** — What is physically shipped or contractually delivered; the innermost ring of the Whole Product Model. (Ch5)

**Gorilla** — The #1-share company in a proprietary, high-switching-cost tornado market; captures disproportionate equity value. (App. 1; Moore, *The Gorilla Game*)

**Head pin / head bowling pin** — The first niche knocked over in the bowling-alley sequence; equivalent to the beachhead. (Ch3)

**High-risk, low-data decision** — The condition under which the beachhead is selected. The wrong response is to clutch for numerical analyst data; the right response is informed intuition. (Ch4)

**Hockey stick forecast** — The conventional VC-friendly revenue curve (slow → sharp inflection → indefinite hypergrowth). Almost always wrong; the staircase is reality. (Ch8 / Conclusion)

**Informed intuition** — Right-brain decision-making based on a handful of memorable archetypes (customer scenarios), not statistical samples. The recommended tool for beachhead selection. (Ch4)

**Innovators / Technology enthusiasts** — The ~2.5% gatekeepers who pursue technology for its own sake. Endorse, not buy in large numbers; their approval legitimizes the product to later groups. (Ch1, Ch2)

**Laggards / Skeptics** — The ~16% segment that resists technology adoption; useful only as critics. (Ch1)

**Main Street** — The fifth and final market state — saturated, cyclical, low-growth. Two viable strategies: operational excellence (low-cost commodity) or customer intimacy ("whole product + 1"). (App. 1)

**Mainstream market** — Post-chasm market dominated by pragmatists and conservatives. Defined by whole-product completeness and market-leader pricing. (Ch1, Ch2)

**Market** (Moore's definition) — A self-referencing set of customers buying the same kind of product for the same kind of need. *Self-reference is the operative test.* (Ch2)

**Market alternative** — In "creating the competition," the incumbent vendor the customer already buys from; defines the budget you intend to preempt. (Ch6)

**Market-centric value attributes** — Whole product completeness, UX, standards compatibility, whole-product price, situational value, fit for purpose. The pragmatist's value language. (Ch6)

**Market Development Strategy Checklist** — Nine-factor scoring template for evaluating beachhead candidates: Target Customer, Compelling Reason to Buy, Whole Product, Competition (four showstoppers) + Partners and Allies, Distribution, Pricing, Positioning, Next Target Customer. (Ch4)

**Pioneer vs. Settler** — Pre-chasm vs. post-chasm organizational personas. Both are essential; asking either to be the other destroys morale. (Ch8 / Conclusion)

**Positioning** — A *noun* — the space a product occupies inside a customer's head. Not the marketing copy used to create it. The single largest influence on the buying decision. (Ch6)

**Positioning Statement Template** — "For [target customer] who is dissatisfied with [market alternative], our product is a [category] that provides [compelling reason to buy]. Unlike [product alternative], we have assembled [whole product features]." (Ch6)

**Potential product** — The product's room for growth via third-party extensions and customer customization; outermost ring of the Whole Product Model. (Ch5)

**Pragmatists** — See Early majority.

**Product alternative** — In "creating the competition," another disruptor using the same new technology. Establishes the legitimacy of the technology wave; you differentiate by segment focus. (Ch6)

**Product-centric value attributes** — Cool product, easy to use, elegant architecture, product price, unique functionality. The early-market value language. (Ch6)

**Provocation-based selling** — Variant of solution selling for early-market or pre-aware prospects: provoke the buyer to redirect existing budget toward an unrecognized opportunity or crisis. (Ch7)

**Psychographic profile** — A psychology + demographics blend that produces a distinctive response to discontinuous innovation. (Ch1)

**Pioneers** — see Pioneer vs. Settler.

**Sales 2.0** — Direct-touch digital marketing/sales/service for department-manager buyers. Sits between Web self-service and direct sales. (Ch7)

**Solution selling** — Tailoring the whole product to a specific prospect's needs; standard direct-sales motion. (Ch7)

**Staircase revenue curve** — The real shape of high-tech revenue development: rapid niche spurts separated by chasm- or transition-period plateaus. The honest replacement for the hockey stick. (Ch8 / Conclusion)

**Target Customer Characterization** — Formal process of generating 20–50 one-page customer/use-case scenarios, distilling to 8–10 distinct archetypes for scoring. (Ch4)

**Technology Adoption Life Cycle (TALC)** — The bell curve of five psychographic segments (innovators → early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards) responding to a discontinuous innovation. (Ch1)

**Tipping point** (consumer) — The moment in the Four Gears model where the engagement system restabilizes around a new status quo and starts to pull adoption forward. Visible only in hindsight. (App. 2)

**Tornado** — The fourth market state — hypergrowth phase where whole sectors simultaneously adopt the technology as must-have infrastructure. (App. 1)

**Two-tier distribution** — Distributor (vendor-facing, holds inventory) + manufacturers' rep (customer-facing). Channel for design engineers and component sales. (Ch7)

**URL model** — "Ubiquity now, Revenue Later." Consumer-internet monetization pattern; opposite of pay-as-you-go B2B. (App. 2)

**Use case** — A specific, named instance of how the product would be bought and used. The unit of scenario analysis. (Ch4)

**Value-Added Reseller (VAR)** — Local reseller providing post-sales integration, support, and customization. Trusted-advisor channel for small business owner-operators. (Ch7)

**Value-based pricing** — Pricing as a fraction of the strategic value delivered; characteristic of visionary buying. (Ch7)

**Visionaries** — See Early adopters.

**Web-based self-service** — Transactional digital sales channel for end-user buyers; often combined with freemium. (Ch7)

**Whole product** — The complete set of products and services required for the customer to fully realize the buying objective. Four layers: generic, expected, augmented, potential. The defining buying object for pragmatists. (Ch5)

**Whole product + 1** — Main Street value-added offer that bolts onto the commodity base with a small price premium but a much larger margin premium. (App. 1)

**Window of opportunity** — The period during which a tech leader can lock in the bell curve before the next innovation displaces it. (Ch1)

**Wiring the marketplace** — Creating a market in which your product is the only reasonable buying proposition, by combining a compelling reason to buy with a monopolized whole product. (Ch5)
