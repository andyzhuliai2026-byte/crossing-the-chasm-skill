# Chapter 5: Assemble the Invasion Force

## Core Idea
Marketing is **warfare, not wordfare**. The way to win the pragmatist is to engineer a marketplace where your product is the only reasonable buying choice — "wire the marketplace" with a **whole product** that completely solves the target customer's broken process. The generic product you ship is only part of the offer.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Whole Product Model** (Theodore Levitt, *The Marketing Imagination*; extended by Bill Davidow, *Marketing High Technology*). Four concentric levels:
  1. **Generic product** — what is in the box / under the purchasing contract.
  2. **Expected product** — *minimum* configuration needed to achieve the buying objective (e.g., a tablet needs Wi-Fi or cellular to be useful).
  3. **Augmented product** — *maximum* support for the buying objective (apps, services, integrations, training).
  4. **Potential product** — room for growth as third parties and customizations extend the ecosystem.
- **Whole Product Importance by Segment**: Importance of outer rings grows left-to-right across the TALC.
  - Tech enthusiasts: cobble their own whole product (motto: "real techies don't need whole products").
  - Visionaries: accept responsibility for assembling it (drives the systems integrator industry).
  - **Pragmatists**: refuse to buy until the whole product is in place — and it must be visibly the *market-leading* one.
  - Conservatives: want it preassembled, single-function, refrigerator-simple.
- **"Pragmatists evaluate and buy whole products."** The most important sentence in chasm marketing. Once 2+ generic products exist, additional R&D on the generic product yields diminishing returns; investment shifts to expected/augmented/potential layers.
- **Wiring the Marketplace**: For a given target customer and application, create a marketplace where *your product is the only reasonable buying proposition*. Two requirements: (1) understand what the whole product consists of, (2) organize the marketplace to deliver it around your offer.

## Key Concepts
- **Whole product planning** — the centerpiece of market domination strategy; map every product, service, partner, and integration the target customer needs end-to-end.
- **Partners and allies** (vs. strategic alliances): partners are recruited around a *specific use case* to fill specific gaps in the whole product. Strategic alliances in the abstract are typically "frothy and feckless" — the whole-product test is the actual basis for partnership.
- **Generic product battle vs. whole product battle**: Disruptive innovations win the early market on the *generic* product (the cool new thing). Mainstream markets are won on the *whole* product — even if the generic product is inferior (Oracle's SQL DB wasn't best; it had best whole product around it).
- **Two whole-product scenarios when crossing**:
  - **Competitive crossing** (vs. installed leader) — like invading Normandy; need a 10× value prop on a disruption the incumbent can't counter.
  - **Greenfield crossing** (no incumbent) — like colonists at Plymouth Rock; need to invent the market and survive cash burn until self-sufficient.

## Mental Models
- **Whole product = the customer's complete success path**, not just your shippable artifact. Work backward from the customer's use case, fill blanks with R&D / partner / acquisition / alliance.
- **The whole product is your responsibility regardless of who builds it**. "If you leave your customer's success to chance, you are giving up control over your own destiny." Pre-chasm: visionaries backfill it. Post-chasm: third parties build it. **In the chasm: only you can build it.**
- **Aruba vs. Cisco case**: 400× size disadvantage. Used 802.11n disruption + college/university market (BYOD before BYOD) + relentless whole-product completion (AirWave for legacy mgmt, Active Directory integration, Bradford for security, Video Furnace for video). No single piece a showstopper for Cisco, but in aggregate too focused for a giant to mimic.
- **Lithium case (greenfield)**: Online communities for customer-enabled tech support; no installed competitor. Won pragmatists by building tribal knowledge bases, mobile support, CRM integration — all augmented-product investments that signal whole-product completeness.

## Anti-patterns
- **"Tossing bales of hay off the back of a truck"**: Shipping a great generic product and hoping third parties rally around. They won't — pragmatists won't move until they see a clear leader, and partners won't bet on a non-leader.
- **Strategic alliances without a specific whole-product gap**: Glossy press releases, no customer impact. Always anchor partnerships to a missing whole-product element.
- **Investing R&D in the generic product when whole-product completeness is the gating constraint**: Common engineering trap — "if we just make it 30% faster…" — when the actual gap is "no one provides training, support, or integration."
- **Outsourcing the whole product to "future market dynamics"**: In the chasm period, no third party is rational to invest in your ecosystem yet. You must lead.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Pragmatists buy whole products**, not generic products. Internalize this.
2. **Plan the whole product end-to-end** from the target customer's use case, by name.
3. Map every whole-product element to one of four sources: (a) your R&D, (b) acquisition, (c) partner, (d) alliance.
4. **Recruit partners around specific gaps**, not abstract synergies.
5. In the chasm, **you are the only one with motivation to assemble the whole product** — own it.
6. Looking like you are winning the whole-product battle accelerates pragmatist commitment; perception ≈ reality here.
