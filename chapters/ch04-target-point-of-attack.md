# Chapter 4: Target the Point of Attack

## Core Idea
Beachhead selection is a **high-risk, low-data decision** — there is no market share data, no comparable customers, no prior analog. The only effective tool is **informed intuition** built from concrete *target customer scenarios* (use cases), filtered through the Market Development Strategy Checklist.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The High-Risk, Low-Data Decision**: The most important marketing decision of the company must be made with essentially no hard market data. Numerical analyst forecasts ("5% of a $10B market by 2026") are "houses of cards" — useless and dangerous.
- **Informed Intuition**: Right-brain decision-making based on a handful of memorable archetypal *images*, not statistical samples. First rule: "If you can't remember it, don't try, because it's not worth it."
- **Target Customer Characterization**: Build 8–10 (out of 20–50 attempted) one-page **target customer scenarios** — fictional but archetypal use cases. Each scenario has a single page, structured below.
- **Scenario Format**:
  1. **Header**: economic buyer, end user, technical buyer (for B2B: industry, geography, dept, title; for B2C: demographics).
  2. **A Day in the Life (Before)**: scene of the user stuck, with consequences for the economic buyer.
  3. **A Day in the Life (After)**: same user, problem solved by your offer.
  4. **Compelling reason to buy**: explicit economic / strategic value of the move.
- **Market Development Strategy Checklist** (9 factors for scoring scenarios):
  Four **showstopper** factors (any low score eliminates the scenario as a beachhead):
  1. **Target customer** — single identifiable economic buyer, accessible, funded for whole product.
  2. **Compelling reason to buy** — pain so severe the buyer cannot live with it another year.
  3. **Whole product** — can be assembled in ≤3 months, dominate in 12 months.
  4. **Competition** — no one has crossed the chasm here ahead of you ("never attack a fortified hill").
  Five **nice-to-have** factors (low scores survivable but costly):
  5. **Partners and allies** — relationships in place?
  6. **Distribution** — sales channel that can reach this customer?
  7. **Pricing** — fits target's budget and channel margins?
  8. **Positioning** — credible as a provider in this niche?
  9. **Next target customer** — does this beachhead set up the bowling alley?

## Key Concepts
- **Use case** — a specific, named instance of how the product would be bought and used; the unit of scenario analysis.
- **Compelling reason to buy** — not "would be nice to have" but "cannot live with the problem another year." Pragmatists with no compelling reason invite you back for "great presentations" but never buy.
- **Showstopper score** — 1–5 per factor on the four critical factors; total worst = 4, best = 20.
- **End-run** — when a competitor occupies your ideal niche, find an angle of attack they cannot defend (a sub-niche, a different buying center, or an adjacent geography).
- **Whole-product-challenged** — the *best* beachhead scenarios will score weakly on whole product, because if it were easy someone else would have done it. This is the barrier to entry that protects you once you solve it.

## Mental Models
- **Characterizations are stand-ins**, not segments. Gen Z, Henrys (High Earners Not Rich Yet), Dinks, visionaries, pragmatists — all are *poster children*, "data fragments" your right brain can manipulate. Always work from a person, not from a market label.
- **You are not picking a target market; you are picking a target *customer*** — markets are impersonal abstractions; customers have heads, faces, and budgets.
- **The numbers refuge is the trap**: When uncertain, people clutch for analyst spreadsheets. Don't. "When you hear that sort of stuff, exit gracefully, holding on to your wallet."
- **Pragmatist veto on "fortified hill"** (Dick Hackborn, HP): if someone has already taken the niche, all the dynamics you wanted to use are working *for them*, not you.

## Anti-patterns
- **Driving strategy from analyst market-size reports**: They are weighted assumptions stacked on assumptions; useful only at macro level.
- **Defining a target as "all CIOs in the Fortune 500"**: That's a category, not a customer scenario; no archetype, no leverage.
- **Picking the segment with the largest market estimate**: Largest segment usually = most defended; you'll lose. Pick the most painful niche where you can win.
- **Going after >1 beachhead "to be safe"**: Dilutes the whole product, dilutes positioning, dilutes references. The book is emphatic — choose one.
- **Confusing "we like this market" with "this market scores well on the checklist"**: If the team has emotional attachment, run the scoring without naming the scenarios at first.

## Key Takeaways
1. Accept the **low-data condition**; substitute **informed intuition driven by scenarios**.
2. Generate 20–50 target customer scenarios → distill to 8–10 distinct → score against the checklist → commit to one.
3. **Four showstoppers come first** (target customer, compelling reason to buy, whole product, competition); anything else can be fixed with time and money.
4. **Compelling reason to buy is the dominant tiebreaker.** A small niche in unbearable pain beats a large niche in mild pain every time.
5. Always evaluate the **next target customer** — your beachhead must seed the bowling alley.
6. Run scoring in a group — informed consensus, not heroic individual judgment.
