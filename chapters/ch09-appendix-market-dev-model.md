# Appendix 1: The High-Tech Market Development Model

## Core Idea
*Crossing the Chasm* covers only the chasm crossing itself. The complete market-development arc has **five states**: Early Market → Chasm → Bowling Alley → Tornado → Main Street. Each state rewards a fundamentally different strategy; the worst sin is to apply the wrong state's tactics to the current one. (Full treatment in Moore's *Inside the Tornado* and *The Gorilla Game*.)

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Five Market States** (with their dominant logic):
  1. **Early Market**
     - Customers: technology enthusiasts + visionaries.
     - Whole product: incomplete; sold as projects.
     - Channel: direct sales.
     - Pricing: value-based (visionaries pay up for 10× ROI).
     - Competition: status quo (no real competitors yet).
     - Logic: invent, prove, deliver custom outcomes.
  2. **The Chasm**
     - Customers: none (visionaries saturated, pragmatists waiting).
     - Logic: covered exhaustively in Ch3–Ch7.
  3. **The Bowling Alley**
     - Customers: pragmatist niches adjacent to the beachhead, linked by either customer word-of-mouth or partner ecosystem.
     - Whole product: niche-specific completeness.
     - Channel: value-focused, not volume-focused.
     - Pricing: premium above eventual commodity price.
     - Competition: positioned at intersection of "incumbent's domain expertise" + "next-gen product performance."
     - Logic: repeat the chasm-crossing playbook, faster and cheaper, niche by niche.
  4. **The Tornado**
     - State change: water → steam. Whole sectors adopt simultaneously.
     - Target customer becomes *generic*; product is must-have infrastructure (think PCs, smartphones, websites).
     - Whole product: assembled at point of sale; partners adjust offers around your platform.
     - Channel: lowest-cost, furthest-reaching that meets service SLAs.
     - Pricing: priced *up* from commodity floor (no longer *down* from market leader).
     - Competition: price-performance + market-share dominance.
     - Logic: capture #1 share; proprietary tech with switching costs creates "gorilla" outcomes (see *The Gorilla Game*).
  5. **Main Street**
     - Single-digit cyclical growth; first-gen infrastructure deployed across the landscape.
     - Two coexisting strategies:
       - **Operational excellence** → low-cost commodity offer; undifferentiated.
       - **Customer intimacy** → "whole product + 1" extensions priced ~10–15% above base, with 5–10× the margin. Base product often subsidized to drive consumption-based downstream revenue (smartphones being the canonical example).
     - Channel: self-serve preferred (overhead kills the smaller absolute revenue).
     - Competition: incumbency or secondary-attribute differentiation; for core functionality, "good enough is good enough."

## Key Concepts
- **Bowling Alley** — sequence of adjacent niches knocked over in order, each leveraging the previous niche's references / partner ecosystem.
- **Tornado** — hypergrowth phase characterized by mass simultaneous adoption.
- **Gorilla** — the #1-share company in a proprietary, high-switching-cost tornado market; captures disproportionate value (Moore's *Gorilla Game*).
- **Whole product + 1** — Main Street value-added offer that bolts onto the commodity base for an outsized margin.
- **State transition** — the riskiest moments; consensus on *which state you're in* is the hardest team alignment problem.

## Mental Models
- **Each state rewards a different playbook**: Tornado tactics in the Bowling Alley = overshoot, burn cash. Bowling Alley tactics in a Tornado = lose market share to a more aggressive competitor.
- **Coherent bets beat hedging**: "It is better to make a coherent bet, everyone rowing in the same direction, and be wrong (because then you can change course quickly) than to delay or waffle (because you learn nothing and get a suboptimal return at every step of the way)."
- **Ice → water → steam**: chasm-crossing = ice → water (a single niche thaws); tornado = water → steam (whole landscape phase-changes). Reaching Main Street is steam → water again (back to liquid stability).

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating Bowling Alley as Tornado** (pre-tornado): trying to attack the mass market before the whole product can be assembled at point of sale. Ends in stalled sales and burnt capital.
- **Treating Tornado as Bowling Alley** (during tornado): focused niche selling when the market is generic. Competitors with platform strategies capture share and lock in switching costs.
- **Treating Main Street as Tornado** (post-tornado): pushing for double-digit growth from a saturated category. Drives wasteful M&A and "extension" products no one wants.

## Key Takeaways
1. The chasm is one of **five** market states; design strategy around the *current* state.
2. **State changes are the high-leverage decisions** — they require the same kind of all-hands consensus as chasm crossing.
3. **Bowling Alley** is the natural next phase after a successful chasm crossing — plan for it from Day 1.
4. **Tornadoes happen** — when the whole product is generic and the category becomes infrastructure, you must shift to platform / volume / share thinking immediately.
5. **Main Street rewards either operational excellence or customer intimacy**; trying to do both half-heartedly loses to focused competitors.
6. Read *Inside the Tornado* (Moore) and *The Gorilla Game* (Moore, Johnson, Kippola) for the full post-chasm playbook.
