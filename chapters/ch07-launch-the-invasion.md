# Chapter 7: Launch the Invasion

## Core Idea
Distribution and pricing are the two decisions that make direct contact with the new mainstream customer. The chasm-crossing objective dictates both: **secure a customer-oriented distribution channel** that pragmatists trust, and **set distribution-oriented pricing** that motivates that channel to do the hard work of introducing a disruptive innovation.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The #1 corporate objective when crossing the chasm**: secure a distribution channel the mainstream pragmatist is comfortable with. **Before revenues, before profits, before press, before customer satisfaction** — everything else can be fixed later if the channel is established.
- **Five Customer-Oriented Distribution Channels** (each matched to a customer type):
  1. **Direct sales** → Enterprise executives ($100Ks–$Ms; complex systems). Marketing = relationship marketing; selling = solution selling / provocation-based selling; delivery via pro services + SIs. (Salesforce, VMware, WorkDay.)
  2. **Web-based self-service** → End users ($10s–$100s; personal/workgroup). Marketing = promotional + freemium; selling = direct response; support = community + chat. (Often skips the chasm entirely → see Four Gears model in Appendix 2.)
  3. **Sales 2.0** → Department managers (mid-market; mid-cost). Direct-touch marketing/sales/service over digital. (Intacct, IntApp, Rackspace, Box.)
  4. **Two-tier distribution** → Design engineers (component sales; "design wins"). Two-tier = manufacturers' reps + distributors (Avnet, Arrow). (Intel, Broadcom, NVIDIA.)
  5. **Value-added resellers (VARs)** → Small business owner-operators. Vendor owns marketing and most of sales; VAR owns post-sales support and trust. (Infusionsoft, Bill.com, Intuit.)
- **Three Customer-Oriented Pricing Models** (per psychographic):
  - **Visionary** = **value-based pricing**. Price-insensitive; *wants* high price to fund the special service required.
  - **Pragmatist** = **competition-based pricing**. Will pay up to 30% premium for market leader; otherwise discount accordingly.
  - **Conservative** = **cost-based pricing**. Wait until commoditization; reward for buying late.
- **Distribution-Oriented Pricing — Two Tests**:
  - **Priced to sell?** → does price avoid becoming the sales-cycle showstopper? (Visionary pricing is too high for pragmatists; reset to market-leader competition-based level.)
  - **Worthwhile to sell?** → does the channel earn a *disproportionate* margin for absorbing the extra effort of pushing a disruptive innovation? (Phase out the premium margin as the product becomes established.)

## Key Concepts
- **Customer-oriented distribution** — match channel to customer type, not to internal preference.
- **Distribution-oriented pricing** — during chasm, price exists to *motivate the channel*, not to satisfy customer or investor.
- **Relationship marketing** — invite-only thought-leadership events for senior execs → personal contact → referral into the org. Standard enterprise marketing front-end.
- **Solution selling** — tailoring the whole product to a prospect's specific needs.
- **Provocation-based selling** — when the prospect doesn't yet recognize the need, provoke them to redirect existing budget toward an unseen opportunity or impending crisis.
- **Freemium** — give the core away, monetize via upsell. Common in self-service end-user channels.
- **Sales 2.0** — fully digital direct-touch (marketing automation alerts → human salesperson via email/chat/voice → webinar/demo → close). Sits between self-service and direct sales.
- **Design win** — engineer's selection of a component triggers a contract negotiation with purchasing; the front-end of two-tier distribution.

## Mental Models
- **Pricing carries a message**: In the mainstream, price *signals* market position. The only acceptable signal is "market leader." Therefore pricing = function of competitive set defined in Ch6, plus channel-motivation premium.
- **Vendor-oriented pricing (cost+margin) is the *least* sound basis for chasm pricing**: It is internally focused at exactly the moment you must be externally focused. Use it only as a sanity check.
- **Each channel implies a transaction count**: $10M ARR can come from 1–2 OEM design wins, 20–40 direct enterprise deals, 200–400 Sales 2.0 deals, 2,000+ VAR deals, or 20,000+ consumer subs. Channel choice cascades into the entire sales-funnel philosophy (top vs. middle vs. bottom emphasis).
- **You typically get only one chance to establish a channel.** Switching channels mid-flight is possible but disruptive — pick deliberately.

## Anti-patterns
- **Pricing for early-market visionaries when selling to pragmatists**: Visionaries wanted *extra* price to fund custom service. Pragmatists hear the same price as "too expensive — go away." Reset to competition-based.
- **Pricing too low to motivate the channel**: Insufficient margin → channel ignores you in favor of established offerings with proven pull-through. Disruptive innovation requires a *premium* channel reward.
- **Choosing the channel from the inside out** (cost of sales, headcount preferences) instead of from the customer in.
- **Building direct sales when the target is end users / small businesses**: economics never close — the math demands self-service or VAR.
- **Assuming the channel "knows how to sell" your product**: When channels push back with pricing/positioning feedback, vendors often blame the channel. Almost always the channel is right.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Channel is the #1 priority. Pick the one that fits the customer**, not the one that fits your org chart.
2. Five channels, five customer types — match them precisely (Direct / Web self-service / Sales 2.0 / Two-tier / VAR).
3. **Pricing during the chasm exists to motivate the channel** — give them a disproportionate margin for taking the risk with you.
4. Set price at **market-leader premium** vs. the competitive set established in Ch6 (typically ~30% premium for the leader).
5. **Vendor-oriented pricing is the weakest input** during chasm-crossing. Use externally anchored pricing (customer + channel + competition).
6. Distribution choice is largely **one-shot** for the chasm period — get it right the first time.
