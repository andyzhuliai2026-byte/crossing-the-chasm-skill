# Chapter 6: Define the Battle

## Core Idea
"Any force can defeat any other force — if it can define the battle." Pragmatists refuse to buy without a *visible competitive set*. Coming out of an early market with no real competitors, you must **create the competition** — anchor your offer between a **market alternative** (someone the customer already buys from for the budget you want) and a **product alternative** (another disruptor using the same new technology).

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Create the Competition**: The single most important marketing communication decision in chasm-crossing. Position your product within a buying category the pragmatist already credits, populated by competitors the pragmatist already recognizes — then make yourself the obvious choice.
  - **Market alternative**: An incumbent the customer has been buying from for years. The budget you intend to preempt. Their stubborn limitation is *your* disruptive opening.
  - **Product alternative**: Another company harnessing the same disruptive innovation. Their existence legitimizes the new tech wave. You differentiate by *segment-specific focus*.
- **The Competitive Positioning Compass**: 2×2 model of *who you're talking to* × *what value domain matters to them*.
  - **X-axis (audience)**: specialists (left, tech/product focused) → generalists (right, market/company focused).
  - **Y-axis (attitude)**: skeptical (top) → supportive (bottom).
  - Four quadrants → four positioning stages:
    1. **Skeptical specialist** (tech enthusiast) → "Name it and frame it" (technology-domain credibility).
    2. **Supportive specialist** (visionary) → "Who for and what for" (product-domain credibility).
    3. **Skeptical generalist** (pragmatist) → "Competition and differentiation" (market-domain credibility).
    4. **Supportive generalist** (conservative) → "Financials and futures" (company-domain credibility).
  - Crossing the chasm = the unnatural move from quadrant 2 (visionary support) back to quadrant 3 (pragmatist skepticism).
- **Product-Centric vs. Market-Centric Value Attributes**:
  - Product-centric: cool product, easy to use, elegant architecture, product price, unique functionality.
  - Market-centric: most complete whole product, solid user experience, compatibility with standards, whole product price, situational value, fit for purpose.
  - **Chasm crossing requires the rhetorical shift from product-centric to market-centric.**
- **The Elevator Pitch / Positioning Statement Template**:
  > **For** (target customers — beachhead segment only) <br>
  > **Who are dissatisfied with** (the current *market alternative*) <br>
  > **Our product is a** (product category) <br>
  > **That provides** (compelling reason to buy). <br>
  > **Unlike** (the *product alternative*), <br>
  > **We have assembled** (key whole product features for your specific application).

## Key Concepts
- **Positioning** is a *noun*: a space inside the customer's head, not a verb you do at them.
- **Positioning is the largest single influence on the buying decision** — evaluations are mostly rationalizations of pre-established positioning.
- **Positioning exists in people's heads, not in your words** — frame in words the customer already uses.
- **People are conservative about positioning changes** — demand the least change from the existing mental model.
- **Easy to buy, not easy to sell** — the goal of positioning. Customers resist selling, enjoy buying.
- **The elevator test** — if you can't explain in two sentences during an elevator ride, you fail. Venture capitalists use it as a literal investment filter.
- **The four-stage positioning ladder** (parallel to the Compass quadrants): Name & frame → Who-for-what-for → Competition & differentiation → Financials & futures.

## Mental Models
- **Box vs. Dropbox example**: Box created its competition by anchoring against Dropbox (the market alternative — consumer-grade file sharing infiltrating enterprises) and other enterprise content vendors (the product alternative). Position = "Dropbox-easy, enterprise-grade." This let IT see Box as a *replacement category* with a category, not a niche.
- **Two-sentence positioning is a feature, not a constraint**: What you *cut* from the statement defines what you stand for. Verinata cut "cheapest." HANA cut "any non-retail use case." Those cuts are the positioning.
- **The claim is not the tagline**: Marketing teams own the claim; ad agencies own the tagline. If the ad's point ≠ the claim's point, change the ad, not the claim.
- **Failed positioning = unable to see yourself from the audience's point of view**: All four positioning stages live in *their* head, not yours.

## Anti-patterns
- **Self-serving competitive set**: Claiming you are "the best marketing consultant with a PhD in Renaissance English." Credible — but neither attractive nor relevant.
- **No competitive set at all**: "We have no competition." Pragmatists hear this as "this isn't a real market yet." Refusing to name competitors = refusing to be a real buying option.
- **Pitching a product-centric story to pragmatists**: "Cool product, elegant architecture, unique functionality" → falls flat with skeptical generalists who want whole-product / market-leadership signals.
- **Adding more claims to make the pitch stronger**: Each extra claim dilutes positioning. People will not let you take up more head-space — they assign you one shorthand reference. Pick it deliberately or the market (including competitors) picks for you.
- **Tagline-driven positioning**: Outsourcing claim development to ad creatives.

## Key Takeaways
1. **You must have visible competition** — if it doesn't exist, you create it via market alternative + product alternative.
2. **Market alternative** anchors the *budget* you're preempting; **product alternative** anchors the *technology wave* you're riding.
3. **Pragmatists buy in the lower-right quadrant** (skeptical generalist) of the Positioning Compass — they want market and company credibility.
4. Crossing the chasm = pivoting your positioning rhetoric from **product-centric** to **market-centric**.
5. Use the **two-sentence template** as the master claim governing all marcomm.
6. Pass the **elevator test** — if you cannot, your strategy is not investable, your marcomm will fragment, and your partners will not commit.
